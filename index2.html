ALL ABOUT THE PERIODIC TABLE

Basic Info

The periodic table, also known as the periodic table of elements, is a tabular display of the chemical elements, which are arranged by atomic number, electron configuration, and recurring chemical properties. The structure of the table shows periodic trends. The seven rows of the table, called periods, generally have metals on the left and nonmetals on the right. The columns, called groups, contain elements with similar chemical behaviours. Six groups have accepted names as well as assigned numbers: for example, group 17 elements are the halogens; and group 18 are the noble gases. Also displayed are four simple rectangular areas or blocks associated with the filling of different atomic orbitals.

History of the periodic table

The history of the periodic table began in 1661 when Boyle defined an element as "those primitive and simple Bodies of which the mixt ones are said to be composed, and into which they are ultimately resolved." In 1789, French chemist Antoine Lavoisier wrote Traité Élémentaire de Chimie (Elementary Treatise of Chemistry), which is considered to be the first modern textbook about chemistry. Lavoisier defined an element as a substance that cannot be broken down into a simpler substance by a chemical reaction.This simple definition served for a century and lasted until the discovery of subatomic particles. Lavoisier's book contained a list of "simple substances" that Lavoisier believed could not be broken down further, which included oxygen, nitrogen, hydrogen, phosphorus, mercury, zinc and sulfur, which formed the basis for the modern list of elements. Lavoisier's list also included 'light' and 'caloric', which at the time were believed to be material substances. He classified these substances into metals and nonmetals. While many leading chemists refused to believe Lavoisier's new revelations, the Elementary Treatise was written well enough to convince the younger generation. However, Lavoisier's descriptions of his elements lack completeness, as he only classified them as metals and non-metals. In 1808-10, British natural philosopher John Dalton published a method by which to arrive at provisional atomic weights for the elements known in his day, from stoichiometric measurements and reasonable inferences. Dalton's atomic theory was adopted by many chemists during the 1810s and 1820s. In 1815, British physician and chemist William Prout noticed that atomic weights seemed to be multiples of that of hydrogen. In 1817, German physicist Johann Wolfgang Döbereiner began to formulate one of the earliest attempts to classify the elements. In 1829, he found that he could form some of the elements into groups of three, with the members of each group having related properties. He termed these groups triads. Definition of Triad law:-"Chemically analogous elements arranged in increasing order of their atomic weights formed well marked groups of three called Triads in which the atomic weight of the middle element was found to be generally the arithmetic mean of the atomic weight of the other two elements in the triad.

Groups in the periodic table

There are 18 groups in the periodic table. They are:

Group 1(or IA): Alkali metals

  • They are light and soft metals
  • they show valency 1+
  • Highly reactive & electropositive
  • good conductors of heat and electricity
  • Strong reducing agents

Group 2(or IIA): alkaline earth metals

  • They are found in the earth's crust.
  • Reactive, however less than group 1.
  • metals having 2 valence electrons.
  • Also good coductors of heat and electricity
  • Strong reducing agents

Groups 3 to 12(IIIB to IIB) are not disscussed. Please check out the site given below for information about these metals.

Transition metals link

Group 13(IIIA): Boron family

  • They consist of one metalliod(Boron) and four metals
  • They have 3 valence electrons

Group 14(IVA): Carbon family

  • They consist of 1 non metal(Carbon), 2 metalliods(Si and Ge) 2 metals(Sn and Pb)
  • They have 4 electrons in their outer shell

group 17(VIIA): Halogens

  • They have 7 electrons in their outermost shell
  • They consist of all halogens
  • Strong oxidising agents
  • Bad or non conductors of electricity

group 18(O): Noble Gases

  • They consist of inert gases
  • They are form stable octets by having 8 valence electrons
  • They do not react with other elements

Periodic Properties

i) Atomic Size(or radius)

Atomic size is defined as:

The distance between the centre of nucleus of an atom and its outermost shell.

Factors on which Atomic size depends:

If Number of shells increases, Atomic size Increases

If Nuclear charge Increases, atomic size Decreases

ATOMIC SIZE DECREASES ACROSS A PERIOD

ATOMIC SIZE INCREASES DOWN A GROUP

ii)Metallic charecter

Metallic charecter is defined as:

Those elements, which have a tendency to lose their valence electrons and form cations are called metals.

Metallic charecter depens on the:

a) Atomic Size

• If atomic size is more, then the outermost shell is farther away and thus experiences less nuclear pull.
• As a result, electron(s) can be removed easily.

GREATER THE ATOMIC SIZE, MORE IS THE METTALIC CHARACTER

b) Nuclear charge

• If nuclear charge is more, then the force exerted on the outermost shell is more.

• this makes it difficult to remove electron(s) from the outermost shell.
GREATER THE NUCLEAR CHARGE, LESS IS THE METALLIC CHARACTER

Mettalic Charecter decreases across a period

Mettalic charecter increases down the group

c) Ionisation potential

Ionisation potential(henceforth referrd to as IP) is defined as:

The energy required to remove from an electron from a neutral isolated gaseous ion is called Ionisation potential

IP is measured in the si system as kilojoule per mole(kJ/mol). Other units are electron volts per atom(eV/atom)

IP depends on:

i)Atomic size
•Greater the Atomic size, less is the force of attraction, as
•The electrons of the outermost shell lie further away from the nuclus, thus making the removal easier.

ii) Nuclear Charge
•Greater the nuclear charge, greater is the attraction for the electrons in the outermost shell
•Thus, the electrons are more firmly attacted and requires more energy to be replaced


IP INCREASES across a period
IP DECREASES down a group
